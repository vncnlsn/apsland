Core Services

What APS is known for

These are the services at the center of what we do — the work where our experience is deepest and where the quality of execution matters most to a project's outcome.

Seismic Permit Acquisition

Primary

We manage the end-to-end process of acquiring landowner access permits for 2D and 3D seismic surveys — coordinating with surface owners, mineral rights holders, and state agencies. This includes explaining the project, negotiating terms, documenting agreements, and confirming that permits are in place before crews move in.

  • Surface and mineral owner contact
  • Permit negotiation and execution
  • Agency permitting (BLM, state agencies, railroad, highway)
  • Permit tracking and compliance documentation

Landowner Relations & Field Oversight

Primary

We don't stop at the signed permit. APS agents are on-site during active field operations to verify that crews are following agreed access routes, respecting property boundaries, and honoring any specific conditions the landowner negotiated. If something goes wrong, we're already there.

  • Pre-entry landowner coordination
  • On-site field monitoring during survey operations
  • Damage identification and claim documentation
  • Post-project restoration verification
  • Settlement negotiation for access claims

Title Research & Courthouse Records

Primary

We trace surface ownership and mineral rights for every parcel along a project route — working directly through county courthouses, tax records, and deed books. Accurate research at the start prevents the kind of access disputes that stop a project mid-survey.

  • Surface owner identification and verification
  • Mineral rights ownership research
  • Deed and chain-of-title examination
  • Parcel mapping and land description verification
  • Title curative work for problematic parcels

How a Project Works

The sequence, from first research to final restoration.

Every seismic or infrastructure project follows the same general sequence. Here's where APS is involved at each stage — and what that means in practice.

1

Project Scoping & Design Review

Before any landowner contact happens, we review the proposed survey area or corridor to identify land status, ownership complexity, and any parcels likely to require extra time. This shapes the permit schedule so the client isn't caught off guard by a difficult parcel late in the program.

Typical duration: 1–3 weeks depending on acreage and data availability.

2

Title Research & Ownership Verification

We work through county courthouse records to confirm who owns each surface parcel and, where applicable, who holds the mineral rights. This step also catches title defects — things like missing heirs, incomplete deeds, or split ownership — before they become field problems.

Run in parallel with landowner contact wherever possible to compress the schedule.

3

Landowner Contact & Permit Negotiation

Agents make personal contact with each landowner — by phone and in person. We explain the project in plain terms, answer their questions, and negotiate access conditions. Most landowners have reasonable concerns that can be addressed directly. The ones that can't are flagged early so the client can decide how to proceed.

We document every contact attempt and conversation for the client's records.

4

Agency & Regulatory Permitting

In addition to landowner permits, many projects require approval from state agencies, BLM, county road departments, railroads, or utilities. We identify the required permits early and manage the application process to avoid delays once private landowner access is secured.

Agency timelines vary significantly by state and permit type — this is factored into the overall schedule from the start.

5

Field Operations & On-Site Oversight

Once crews are in the field, APS agents are on-site to confirm that crews are following agreed access routes and respecting the terms of each permit. If a fence gets damaged, a crop gets driven through, or a gate gets left open, we're already there to document it and address it before the landowner calls anyone.

Field oversight is where a lot of permit companies stop — and where most landowner problems start.

6

Restoration & Project Closeout

After field work is complete, we verify that the property has been restored to the agreed condition, document any remaining damage claims, and negotiate settlements. We don't consider a project closed until the landowner is satisfied and all outstanding items are resolved.

A clean closeout protects the client's ability to go back to the same area in the future.

About APS

Four decades of showing up
and following through.

Allstate Permit Services has been doing this work since 1980 — long before anyone called it "land services." Here's how we got here, and how we work.

How it started

Allstate Permit Services was founded in 1980, during a period when the energy industry was expanding rapidly across the country and the work of securing landowner access — the step that makes any survey or pipeline possible — was still largely improvised. There was no standard playbook. Projects stalled because agents didn't have the right background, because landowners weren't given straight answers, or because nobody stayed on-site long enough to make sure the work matched the agreement.

APS was built to solve that specific problem. Not just to acquire permits, but to manage the relationship between energy companies and the landowners whose property they depend on — doing it carefully, doing it right, and doing it in a way that held up once the crews arrived.

The work is quieter than the projects it enables. You won't see the APS name on a pipeline or a seismic report. But the access agreements, the title research, the signed permits, and the field oversight that allow those projects to run — that's what we've been doing since 1980.

How we work

APS is a mid-sized firm. That's intentional. We're large enough to staff multi-state projects and handle everything a major seismic or infrastructure client needs — and small enough that the people managing your project are the same people you talk to when something comes up.

Our field agents are experienced contractors we've worked with for years. We don't put unfamiliar faces into sensitive landowner situations. The agents who knock on doors have done it before, in similar regions, on similar project types. They know how to explain a vibroseis survey to a fourth-generation rancher who has every reason to say no.

We also stay present during fieldwork — something most permit firms don't do. If a crew deviates from the agreed access route, damages a fence line, or leaves a gate open, we hear about it before the landowner calls your office. That part of our work doesn't show up on a service list, but it matters more than almost anything else we do.

What we stand for

Landowners aren't obstacles. They're people with a legal right to understand what's happening on their property — and a legitimate interest in being treated with respect. We've always operated on that principle. It's why the same clients have been calling us back for decades.

When a landowner asks what the crew is going to do, what they'll leave behind, and what happens if something goes wrong — they deserve a real answer. We give one. Honesty is the foundation of every access agreement we've ever negotiated, and it's the only reason those agreements hold up in the field.

How We Operate

The things that don't change.

Clients, project types, and states come and go. These three things have defined how we work since 1980.

I

Accuracy before speed

Getting the research right at the start is faster than fixing access disputes mid-project. We don't cut corners on title research, and we don't hand over a permit package until we're confident it'll hold up in the field.

II

Honest with landowners

A landowner who understands exactly what's happening on their property — and trusts that what they're told is what will happen — is a landowner who says yes. We've never built our practice on pressure or fine print.

III

Present through closeout

A signed permit isn't a finished job. We stay present during field operations and don't consider a project closed until the property is restored and every outstanding claim is settled. The client's reputation depends on it.
